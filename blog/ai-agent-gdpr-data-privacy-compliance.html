All of this constitutes "personal data" under most privacy regulations, which means your handling of it may be subject to legal requirements depending on your location and your customers' locations.

The Key Privacy Regulations to Know

GDPR (European Union)

If you have customers in the EU, GDPR applies. It requires you to have a lawful basis for processing personal data, be transparent about how data is used, allow individuals to request deletion of their data, and maintain appropriate security measures. Using a cloud AI that processes EU customer data on servers outside the EU adds complexity.

LGPD (Brazil)

Brazil's Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados is similar to GDPR in its requirements. It applies to any business processing personal data of individuals in Brazil, regardless of where the business is located. For Brazilian small businesses, it's particularly relevant.

CCPA (California) and Similar US Laws

California's Consumer Privacy Act gives consumers rights over their data and requires transparency about collection and use. Similar laws are emerging in other US states.

The Cloud AI Privacy Problem

When you use a cloud-based AI agent, your customer's personal data is being processed by a third-party service provider. Under GDPR and similar regulations, this makes that provider a "data processor" — and you need to have a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with them. You also need to disclose to customers that their data is processed by third parties.

Most small businesses using cloud AI tools haven't set up DPAs with their AI providers. This creates a compliance gap that, while unlikely to be actively enforced for small businesses, represents a real risk.

Practical Steps for Small Businesses

Regardless of which AI tool you use, these practices help with data privacy compliance:

  1. Be transparent: If customers ask whether they're talking to an AI, tell them honestly
  2. Minimize data collection: Only collect what you actually need to serve the customer
  3. Limit retention: Don't store conversation data longer than necessary
  4. Allow deletion requests: Have a process to delete a customer's data if they request it
  5. Review your AI tool's privacy policy: Understand what they do with the data you share with them

A Practical Note

This article provides general information, not legal advice. Privacy law is complex and evolves constantly. If your business handles significant volumes of personal data or operates in a highly regulated industry, consulting a privacy lawyer is worthwhile. For most small businesses using AI agents for routine customer communication, the practical priority is choosing tools that minimize data exposure — which points toward local AI — and being transparent with customers about how your AI assistance works.
